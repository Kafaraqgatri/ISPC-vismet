Beyond Anomaly: Integrative Inference in Hybrid Visual Metaphor Processing

How do people understand that a cracked heart depicts heartbreak rather than a damaged object? Cognitive scientists suggest that metaphors convey meaning by applying properties from a concrete source domain (such as breaking something) to a target domain (such as relationships, Lakoff & Johnson, 1980). Thus, the meaning of “heartbreak” draws on the concrete experience of something breaking and relates it to the emotional distress associated with loss or disappointment. Although a person’s heart is not literally broken, the metaphor efficiently communicates a complex internal state. Metaphoric meaning is pervasive in language, where words and sentence structures guide the mappings between source and target domains (Steen, 2007). However, similar mappings can also be conveyed visually. In visual metaphors, meaning is conveyed through pictorial elements rather than linguistic input: this comes with additional processes. Viewers must select relevant visual features, suppress irrelevant ones, and integrate them with stored conceptual knowledge to infer the intended meaning (Forceville, 1996, 2008; Phillips & McQuarrie, 2004). However, a cracked heart image, for instance, visually instantiates the same metaphorical mapping conveyed by the verbal expression, just with visual instead of linguistic cues.

The present study focuses specifically on hybrid visual metaphors, where source and target domain are blended within a single image (such as a heart shape with a crack). In the visual metaphor literature, researchers have identified several structural templates through which pictorial metaphors are constructed. Phillips and McQuarrie (2004) distinguished three basic visual structures: juxtapositions, in which source and target objects are placed side by side; fusions or hybrids, in which two objects from different domains are merged into a single composite entity; and replacements, in which one object appears in a visual context that evokes a second, absent object. Forceville (1996) introduced a parallel typology, referring to fusions as "MP2 metaphors" (later termed "hybrid metaphors" in Forceville, 2008) and to replacements as "MP1" or "contextual" metaphors. In a hybrid visual metaphor, the two domains (source and target) are graphically integrated into one non-existent object. For example, an image that merges a hammer and a flute into a single artifact, so that the resulting figure is simultaneously recognizable as elements of both source and target domains. Because the object's identity is inherently ambiguous, the viewer cannot simply identify what is depicted but must actively construct a cross-domain mapping to resolve the incongruity. The present study uses hybrid visual metaphors as stimuli because their structural integration of source and target within a single pictorial unit creates a controlled context in which cross-domain (from source to target) mapping is required, making them well suited for examining the neural time course of metaphorical processing.

Recent theoretical work has sought to move beyond simple typologies and develop more principled accounts of how visual structures give rise to metaphorical interpretation. A central contribution in this regard is Schilperoord's (2018) incongruity-based framework, developed within the Visual Metaphor: Structure and Process (Steen, 2018). Schilperoord argues that no image is inherently metaphorical; rather, images may be visually incongruent, deviating from viewers' expectations and understanding of reality, and metaphor is one of several conceptual structures that can resolve such incongruity. He proposes that all visual incongruities can be analyzed in terms of two components: an Establisher (visual input that activates a cognitive model or expectation) and a Violator (visual input that conflicts with that model). Two broad classes of incongruity emerge from this analysis. Two-model incongruities employ separate cognitive models for the Establisher and Violator, and can be further distinguished by their assembling operation: Insert (placing an entity from one model into a scene governed by another), Substitute (replacing an expected entity with an unexpected one), Merge (graphically integrating elements from two models into a hybrid object), Ambiguate (depicting an object that fully satisfies the visual parameters of two different models simultaneously), and Transmute (metamorphosing one object into another). One-model incongruities, by contrast, involve distortion or erasure of an entity that belongs to the scene's own cognitive model.

Schilperoord (2018) demonstrates that the same incongruity structure can give rise to different interpretive outcomes depending on how the message's topic relates to the incongruent element. When the topic is identical to the Violator or to a substituted entity, metaphorical mapping typically resolves the incongruity. For example, when a pack of custard substitutes a wine bottle in a wine box, and the advertisement is about custard, viewers construe the metaphor “Custard as wine”. However, when the topic is not identical to any component of the incongruity, other resolution types are required, such as categorical contrasts or range relations. This analysis carries an important implication for processing research: the cognitive effort involved in resolving a visual metaphor is not simply a response to anomaly detection but reflects the specific inferential work of linking incongruity to topic and constructing an appropriate cross-domain mapping. The title of the present study "Beyond Anomaly" reflects this theoretical insight: we propose that the ERP signatures elicited by hybrid visual metaphors index not merely the detection of visual anomaly but the integrative inferential processes through which metaphorical meaning is constructed.

Complementing the structural analysis, empirical work within the same research program has examined how perceptual features of visual metaphors influence their processing. Van Weelden, Maes, and Schilperoord (2018) investigated the role of shape similarity in juxtaposed visual metaphors, drawing on Humphreys and Forde's (2001) Hierarchical Interactive Theory (HIT). According to HIT, when an object is perceived, its visual features activate a structural description that simultaneously co-activates structural descriptions of similarly shaped objects; these in turn spread activation to stored semantic knowledge. Van Weelden et al. (2018) tested this mechanism across two studies and found that when two objects from different conceptual domains shared similar shapes, participants were faster to identify correspondences between them and produced both more attributive correspondences (based on shared visual features) and more relational correspondences (based on shared conceptual properties such as function). Shape similarity thus appears to serve as a perceptual bootstrap for metaphorical mapping: even when no ready-made conceptual category links two objects, their visual resemblance facilitates the discovery of deeper relational connections. These findings suggest that ERP responses to visual metaphors may be modulated not only by the conceptual distance between source and target but also by the degree of perceptual overlap between the depicted objects.

A further factor that shapes visual metaphor processing is conventionality. Bowdle and Gentner's (2005) Career of Metaphor theory holds that novel metaphors are processed through comparison, an effortful alignment of source and target features, whereas conventional metaphors are processed through categorization, in which the source automatically evokes an abstract category (e.g., "rocket" directly activating the category "fast"). Although this theory was developed for verbal metaphor, Hodiamont, Hoeken, and Van Mulken (2018) tested whether conventionality operates similarly across modalities. In their experiment, 80 participants rated 17 metaphors on perceived conventionality, with each metaphor presented either verbally (e.g., "this car is a rocket") or visually (as a juxtaposition of two images). They found that visual metaphors varied significantly in perceived conventionality, and that verbal and visual conventionality ratings were highly correlated, particularly after removing five items for which modality-specific factors influenced ratings. This result supports Conceptual Metaphor Theory's claim that metaphorical relations are represented at a conceptual level that is largely independent of presentation modality (Lakoff & Johnson, 1980; Holyoak & Thagard, 1995). For the present ERP study, the implication is that familiar and unfamiliar visual metaphors may engage qualitatively different processing mechanisms, a prediction we test directly.

Beyond the work reviewed above, several other lines of research have advanced our understanding of visual metaphor. Van Mulken, Van Hooft, and Nederstigt (2014) examined the relationship between conceptual complexity and viewer appreciation in visual metaphor advertising, finding that moderately complex metaphors yielded the highest appreciation, a "tipping point" beyond which increased complexity led to decreased comprehension and liking. Jeong (2008) investigated whether the persuasive effects of visual metaphors in advertising stem from visual argumentation (the metaphor serving as an implicit argument for a product claim) or from the rhetorical pleasure of processing the metaphor itself, finding evidence for both pathways. Indurkhya and Ojha (2017) explored the asymmetry of visual metaphor interpretation, demonstrating that reversing source and target in a visual metaphor changes the inferred meaning, which supports the view that visual metaphors, like their verbal counterparts, involve directional mappings rather than symmetric comparisons. Together, these studies establish that visual metaphors are processed through mechanisms that are sensitive to structural complexity, directionality, familiarity, and the interplay of perceptual and conceptual features, all factors that may manifest in the temporal dynamics of neural processing.

Despite this growing body of behavioral and theoretical work, relatively little is known about the neural time course of visual metaphor comprehension. Many ERP studies have examined linguistic metaphor processing (e.g., Baiocco et al., 2026), documenting components such as the N400, which indexes semantic integration difficulty, and late positive components associated with reanalysis and meaning construction. However, the extent to which these same neural signatures characterize the processing of visual metaphors remains largely unexplored. The present study uses event-related potentials to examine the cognitive-neural processing of hybrid visual metaphors. We ask two primary questions: first, whether hybrid visual metaphors engage cognitive-neural processes similar to those observed during linguistic metaphor comprehension; and second, whether metaphor familiarity, the visual analogue of conventionality, modulates these neural responses. By situating our ERP investigation within the theoretical and empirical framework reviewed above, we aim to bridge the gap between structural and processing accounts of visual metaphor and to provide neural-level evidence for the integrative inferential processes that these images demand.

1.1. Event-Related Potential correlates of linguistic metaphor 

A growing body of event-related potential (ERP) research has examined how the brain processes linguistic metaphors in real time. A comprehensive synthesis of this literature is a recent review by Baiocco, Kielar, and Lai (2026), who systematically reviewed 62 ERP experiments on verbal metaphor comprehension in neurotypical adults. Their review identified three ERP components that recur across the metaphor literature, the N400, a late positivity (LPC), and a late negativity (LN), and clarifies the stimulus and task factors that modulate each. The present section draws heavily on their findings to establish the ERP landscape that motivates the current study.

The N400 is a negative-going waveform that typically peaks around 200–600 ms after stimulus onset and is sensitive to semantic processing, including difficulty retrieving (Kutas & Federmeier, 2011; Brouwer et al., 2012 or integrating (Osterhout & Holcomb, 1992) meaning in context. This component is not specific to metaphor: it was originally discovered in response to semantic anomalies (Kutas & Hillyard, 1980) and is observed across a wide range of language and non-language tasks involving meaning access.

In metaphor research, the N400 has been the most consistently reported finding: Baiocco et al. (2026) found that 87.1% of the 62 reviewed experiments reported a more negative N400 for metaphors compared with literal controls. This effect is modulated by at least two factors. First, metaphor familiarity matters: among experiments that explicitly included novel metaphors, 84.8% reported an N400 effect relative to literal sentences, whereas among experiments with conventional metaphors, only 54.5% did so. When novel and conventional metaphors were directly compared, 72.2% of experiments found a more negative N400 for novel metaphors. Second, context plays a significant role: among studies using sentential contexts, 89.5% found a metaphor-related N400, and among those using lexical contexts (word pairs), 91.7% did so. However, among studies that provided richer discourse context or semantic priming, only 50% found an N400 effect, suggesting that supportive context can substantially attenuate the processing cost indexed by this component (see also Bambini et al., 2016; Deckert et al., 2021). Baiocco et al. (2026) conclude that the metaphor N400 primarily reflects the effort involved in retrieving and accessing meaning, effort that is greatest when metaphors are novel and presented with limited contextual support. Importantly, these N400 effects appear to reflect graded modulation of a general-purpose semantic retrieval mechanism rather than a metaphor-specific process: novel metaphors simply place greater demands on the same retrieval system that is engaged during all meaningful language comprehension.

The picture becomes more complex, and less consistent, in later time windows (500–1,000 ms post-onset). Baiocco et al. (2026) report that only 20.9% of the 62 reviewed experiments found a late positivity (LPC) for metaphors relative to literal controls, while 37.1% found a late negativity (LN), and 41.9% reported no later effects at all. The LPC, variously labeled P600 or late positive component (LPC) across studies, has been associated in the broader language literature with integration difficulty, reanalysis, or revision of an initial interpretation (Brouwer et al., 2012, 2017; Kuperberg, 2007; Van Petten & Luka, 2012). In the metaphor literature, it has been interpreted as reflecting downstream interpretive processes such as constructing or evaluating a metaphoric meaning, aligning source and target domains, or integrating metaphoric meaning with broader discourse (De Grauwe et al., 2010; Bambini et al., 2016). Based on their review, Baiocco et al. (2026) suggest that two factors most consistently elicit the LPC for metaphors: contextual richness, which supports meaning integration, and stimulus emotionality. For example, Baiocco, Pfeifer, and Lai (2025) found that emotional metaphors elicited a more positive LPC than emotional literal sentences during passive reading, but this effect was absent for emotionally neutral metaphors, suggesting that the LP may index not metaphor processing per se, but the elaborated integration of affective-semantic content that some metaphors happen to carry. The LN, by contrast, has been found more consistently and is more clearly linked to metaphor-relevant processing. Baiocco et al. (2026) associate it with re-analysis or evaluation of stimuli, driven primarily by stimulus novelty and task complexity. Among experiments that explicitly included novel metaphors, 54.5% reported an LN for novel metaphors relative to literal controls, and when novel and conventional metaphors were directly compared, 55.5% found a more negative LN for novel metaphors. No studies found an LPC for novel metaphors compared with conventional ones. The LN has also been shown to increase when tasks demand deeper semantic evaluation: Rataj et al. (2018), for instance, found larger LN effects for novel metaphors during a sensicality judgment task than during passive reading, and Goldstein et al. (2012) demonstrated that requiring participants to verbally explain metaphors shifted the LN pattern, suggesting that this component tracks the depth of interpretive engagement. These late effects, both LPC and LN, likely reflect processing stages that go beyond the initial semantic access indexed by the N400, capturing the inferential and evaluative work involved in constructing a metaphoric interpretation. However, like the N400, these late components are not unique to metaphor: the LPC is widely observed in response to emotional stimuli and syntactic reanalysis across many language tasks, and late negativities appear in studies of aspectual coercion, referential ambiguity resolution, and elaborative semantic processing more broadly (Paczynski et al., 2014; Nieuwland & Van Berkum, 2008). What distinguishes metaphor processing, then, is not the recruitment of qualitatively novel neural mechanisms but rather the particular pattern and intensity with which these general-purpose components are engaged, especially the combination of an N400 reflecting effortful meaning retrieval followed by late components reflecting sustained interpretive elaboration.

Together, these ERP findings from linguistic metaphor research provide a well-characterized temporal framework: an early phase of semantic access and retrieval (N400), followed by a later phase of meaning integration, reanalysis, and interpretive elaboration (LPC/LN). A central open question, however, is whether this same temporal signature characterizes the processing of visual metaphors. As reviewed in the preceding section, visual metaphors, and hybrid visual metaphors in particular, require viewers to resolve pictorial incongruity through cross-domain mapping without the benefit of syntactic cues, explicit copular structures, or the sequential word-by-word unfolding that characterizes verbal metaphor. If the N400 and later components reflect domain-general conceptual operations rather than language-specific processes, they should be elicited by visual metaphors as well. If, on the other hand, these components are tightly coupled to linguistic processing mechanisms, visual metaphors may produce a different neural profile altogether. The present study is designed to adjudicate between these possibilities.

1.2. ERP signature of visual meaning construction

. Research on comics and visual narratives has demonstrated that the brain can recruit ERP components similar to those observed in sentence comprehension when processing structured sequences of images.  Studies using panel-by-panel presentation of visual narratives report N400 effects for semantically unexpected or incongruent panels and P600 like effects for panels that require structural revision or updating of a situation model (Cohn et al., 2012; Cohn & Kuperberg, 2014; Coderre et al., 2020; Cohn, 2021). These findings indicate that when visual input is segmented into sequential frames, as in comics, where illustrators deliberately structure the temporal unfolding of a narrative, meaning construction proceeds over temporally dissociable processing stages that parallel those studied in language. However, it is important to note that these parallels emerge under conditions that share a key structural feature with language: both sentences and comic strips deliver information in a sequential, frame-by-frame or word-by-word format, allowing the brain to generate predictions about upcoming input. Whether similar processing stages characterize the comprehension of single, non-sequential images; such as photographs, paintings, or advertisements; remains a more open question, because such images do not impose a comparable temporal structure on the viewer.

Indeed, there are fundamental differences between how the brain processes sentences and how it processes single images that are important to consider when extending ERP findings across modalities. Language is symbolic and sequential: words are arbitrary symbols whose meanings are retrieved from memory and combined according to grammatical rules, and whether spoken or written, linguistic input is delivered in a largely linear, word-by-word fashion. This sequential structure means that each incoming word can be evaluated against the predictions generated by the preceding context, and it is this process of prediction confirmation or violation that largely drives the N400 and P600 components in language research. Pictures, by contrast, are perceptual and largely simultaneous: a single image presents its contents all at once, and the viewer must extract meaning through visual recognition and spatial analysis rather than through symbolic decoding. Although eye-tracking research shows that viewers do scan images sequentially via fixations and saccades, the order in which elements are attended is not fixed by the stimulus but is determined by the viewer's attentional priorities, the visual salience of the elements, and the task demands. In the case of visual metaphors, particularly hybrid metaphors that merge two objects into a single composite image, it is not known whether viewers process the source domain before the target, or vice versa, or whether both are accessed simultaneously. This stands in sharp contrast to linguistic metaphors such as "this car is a rocket," where the syntactic structure determines the order in which target and source are encountered. These differences in input structure, temporal unfolding, and processing order mean that ERP components observed in visual paradigms should not be assumed to reflect identical cognitive operations to those observed in language, even when they share similar morphologies and time courses.

With these differences in mind, ERP research on object and scene processing provides relevant context for understanding what neural signatures visual metaphors might elicit. Semantically incongruent objects embedded in scenes are known to produce a negativity peaking around 300 ms (the N300), followed by N400-like activity and, in some cases, later positivities (Barrett & Rugg, 1990; McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; West & Holcomb, 2002; Mudrik, Lamy, & Deouell, 2010). Although there is debate about the functional separation between N300 and N400 in the visual modality, ERP negativities in the N300/N400 time range have been linked to difficulty mapping visual input onto stored representations and integrating it with context or expectations (McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; West & Holcomb, 2002; Mudrik et al., 2010). Notably, in picture-based paradigms, these negativities often span a broader time range than the canonical linguistic N400, sometimes extending into a sustained negativity through 700–800 ms. This extended time course likely reflects the fact that visual meaning construction can involve prolonged processing when recognition or interpretation is not immediately achieved; for example, when a stimulus is semantically ambiguous or when the task encourages participants to search for an interpretation rather than simply detect a mismatch. 

A relevant example comes from Mudrik, Lamy, and Deouell (2010), who presented simultaneously viewed scenes containing either congruent or incongruent objects (e.g., a man playing a violin vs. a man "playing" a broomstick). They observed two temporally distinct negativities for incongruent scenes: an anterior negativity emerging around 270 ms and lasting approximately 330 ms, resembling the N300/N400 associated with semantic integration, followed by a later, broadly distributed negativity between 650 and 850 ms, which they linked to late semantic evaluation and response preparation. This dual-negativity profile is relevant to the present study because the task demands are structurally similar: like Mudrik et al., our participants viewed a single, simultaneously presented complex image requiring interpretation rather than a sequence of words or panels that would allow incremental prediction. Under such conditions, the observed negativity may reflect the combined contribution of an early N300/N400 response linked to object recognition and semantic access, and later negative-going activity (sometimes discussed as the N700) associated with imagery, reanalysis, or elaborative meaning construction (McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; West & Holcomb, 2000; Mudrik et al., 2010). In addition, N400 amplitudes in both language and picture processing are known to be modulated by semantic richness factors such as concreteness (Barber, Otten, Kousta, & Vigliocco, 2013; Van Elk, Van Schie, & Bekkering, 2010), an important consideration for visual metaphor research given that metaphoric images necessarily combine concrete visual elements with abstract conceptual content. As Baiocco et al. (2026) note in their review of linguistic metaphor ERPs, concreteness effects can overlap with and potentially confound metaphor effects, and this concern applies equally to visual paradigms.

Late positivities have also been observed in visual processing paradigms. In studies of scene comprehension, positivities emerging after approximately 600 ms have been linked to post-recognition integration and updating processes, particularly when viewers must revise or update their interpretation of a visual event (Cohn et al., 2012; Võ & Wolfe, 2013). In the specific domain of visual metaphor, Ortiz, Grima Murcia, and Fernandez (2017) used ERP to compare hybrid pictorial metaphors with literal images in an advertising context. They reported enhanced P200 activity in posterior regions for metaphoric images, followed by distinct patterns of N400 activity and a late positivity (P600) for metaphors relative to literal images, which they interpreted as reflecting the additional processing demands of cross-domain mapping. However, it should be noted that their study used a small number of trials (12 items), limiting the signal-to-noise ratio and the reliability of the observed effects. More recently, work on combinatorial processing has shown that meaning-building operations can be elicited by pictorial inputs even in the absence of linguistic structure (Bemis & Pylkkänen, 2011, 2013; Parrish & Pylkkänen, 2022), further supporting the view that cross-domain conceptual combination is not restricted to language.

Taken together, this literature suggests that meaning construction in the visual modality,  particularly when it involves incongruity, ambiguity, or the need to construct an interpretation beyond what is immediately given, engages cognitive processes that share temporal and morphological correlates with those observed in the language modality.  Early negativities in the N300/N400 range appear to index difficulty in semantic access and integration, with the potential to extend in time when interpretation is prolonged or uncertain. Later positivities (range?) appear to reflect downstream processes involved in updating, revising, or evaluating meaning. These parallels between visual and language modality motivate the prediction that hybrid visual metaphors, which require viewers to resolve a pictorial incongruity through cross-domain mapping, should elicit both early and late ERP effects.

Importantly, these semantic ERP components are also sensitive to familiarity and conventionality. In the linguistic modality, novel metaphors elicit larger and more consistent N400 effects than conventional ones, suggesting that much of the processing cost attributed to metaphor may reflect unfamiliarity rather than cross-domain mapping per se (Bowdle & Gentner, 2005; Baiocco et al., 2026). In the visual modality, Hodiamont, Hoeken, and Van Mulken (2018) demonstrated that visual metaphors vary in perceived conventionality along a continuum that correlates substantially with verbal conventionality ratings, indicating that familiarity operates across modalities. This raises the possibility that ERP differences between metaphoric and literal images may be partly driven by how well-known a particular source-target pairing is, a factor the present study takes into account.

1.3. Visual metaphors and the role of familiarity

Direct ERP evidence for visual metaphors is limited, and existing findings are often difficult to interpret because metaphoric and literal images frequently differ on familiarity and related dimensions known to modulate semantic processing. One of the few electrophysiological studies to directly address visual metaphors is Ortiz et al. (2017), who examined ERPs elicited by hybrid metaphors (images where source and target domains are blended into a single, unnatural object) and literal images in advertising stimuli. Using a whole-scalp topographic analysis, they reported reliable differences between metaphoric and literal images in three discrete time windows: an early posterior positivity (157–196 ms), which they interpreted as reflecting early perceptual processing of the visual stimulus, a negative-going effect in the N400 range (406–430 ms), interpreted as reflecting the initial semantic incongruity of hybrid metaphors; and a later positivity in the P600 range (580–587 ms), associated with subsequent stages of interpretation or integration. However, Ortiz and colleagues (2017) used only 12 experimental items, which limits the signal-to-noise ratio and the generalizability of their findings.

In addition to Ortiz et al. (2017), two ERP studies have examined the pictorial metaphors in advertising contexts. .Cao et al., (2018presented product-related words followed by metaphoric or literal advertising images and asked participants to judge whether the image matched the intended product message. They found that metaphoric images elicited larger amplitudes in the N1 (100–150 ms), N2 (200–300 ms), and LPC (400–600 ms and 600–1,000 ms) time windows compared to literal images, which they interpreted as an N1–N2–LPC processing pattern specific to pictorial metaphors, beginning with early visual detection, proceeding through incongruity detection in the N2 range, and culminating in extended reanalysis and integration indexed by the LPC. Cao et al. (2023) extended this work by examining how different pictorial metaphor structures (fusions vs. juxtapositions) interact with verbal framing (metaphor "A is B" vs. simile "A is like B"). They found early ERP effects (N1, P2, P3) that varied by pictorial structure and verbalization form, suggesting that the match between visual and verbal framing modulates early perceptual and attentional responses. Across these studies, the pattern of results broadly suggests that visual metaphors engage temporally distinct processing stages, but the specific ERP components and their time courses vary considerably depending on the stimulus format, task, and control conditions employed.

A limitation shared across these studies is that metaphoric and literal images were not matched on familiarity, plausibility, or perceived coherence, dimensions that are known to independently modulate ERP amplitudes, particularly in the N400 and later time windows. This concern is not unique to visual metaphor research. In the linguistic metaphor literature, familiarity and conventionality reliably modulate processing costs: novel or unfamiliar metaphors elicit larger N400 amplitudes than conventional ones, and in the Baiocco et al. (2026) review, novel metaphors produced an N400 relative to literal controls in 84.8% of experiments compared with only 54.5% for conventional metaphors (see also Arzouan et al., 2007; Lai et al., 2009; De Grauwe et al., 2010; Bambini et al., 2016). Similarly, in the visual modality, familiar or expected images tend to elicit reduced negativities compared with unfamiliar or implausible images (Holcomb & McPherson, 1994; Hamm et al., 2002). Hodiamont, Hoeken, and Van Mulken (2018) further demonstrated that visual metaphors vary in perceived conventionality along a continuum that correlates substantially with verbal conventionality ratings, confirming that familiarity operates across modalities.

This means that ERP differences between metaphoric and literal images may be driven not by cross-domain mapping per se, but by the simple fact that metaphoric images are less familiar or more visually unexpected than their literal counterparts. If metaphoric meaning engages the same semantic and interpretive mechanisms across language and vision, then controlling for familiarity at the item level is essential for isolating effects that are genuinely attributable to metaphor processing. The present study addresses this concern by quantifying and modeling familiarity for each stimulus, allowing us to distinguish ERP modulations that track with metaphoricity from those that covary with how well-known or expected a particular image is.

1.4. The current study 

The present study examined the time course of visual metaphor processing using ERPs. Participants viewed three types of images:: hybrid metaphoric images (in which source and target domains are merged into a single composite object, as described in Section 1.0) , literal images depicting plausible non-metaphoric scenes, and anomalous images that were visually coherent but did not afford a metaphorical interpretation (Figure 1). The goal was to explore whether hybrid visual metaphors elicit similar neural signatures to linguistic metaphors when controlling familiarity and sensicality. 

This three-condition design targets two partly separable dimensions of visual meaning construction. First, interpretability/coherence is probed by contrasting anomalous images with the two interpretable conditions (Literal and Metaphor), which should primarily index mismatch-driven difficulty in mapping the image onto stored representations. Second, metaphoric mapping demand is probed by contrasting Metaphor with Literal under conditions of high interpretability, and we further evaluate this contrast with familiarity ratings to reduce confounds from item-level familiarity differences.

Based on prior literature described above, we focused on two time windows. The earlier time window (200–800 ms) was chosen to capture negativities associated with semantic access and integration difficulty in visual processing. This broad window is motivated by evidence that picture-evoked negativities, including the N300 linked to object categorization and the N400 linked to semantic integration, can be sustained and temporally overlapping, particularly when stimuli are semantically incongruent or when the task encourages interpretive processing (McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; West & Holcomb, 2000; Mudrik et al., 2010).  Consistent with this, Mudrik et al. (2010) reported two temporally distinct negativities for simultaneously presented incongruent scenes: an early N300/N400-like effect beginning around 270 ms and a later broadly distributed negativity between 650 and 850 ms, suggesting that visual meaning construction for simultaneously presented stimuli unfolds across approximately this range. The existing visual metaphor ERP studies also report effects in this range: Ortiz et al. (2017) found a negative-going effect at 406–430 ms, and Cao et al. (2018) reported an N2 effect at 200–300 ms followed by LPC activity beginning around 400 ms. The later time window (800–1500 ms) was chosen to capture positivities associated with downstream interpretive processing, including reanalysis, meaning construction, and evaluative processes that follow initial semantic access (Cohn et al., 2012; Võ & Wolfe, 2013; Ortiz et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2018).Fig. 1. We had three predictions. First, in the earlier time window (200–800 ms), anomalous images were expected to elicit larger negativities than literal images, reflecting increased difficulty in semantic access and integration. If metaphoric images also impose additional semantic demands relative to literal images, due to the need for cross-domain integration, they may similarly elicit enhanced negativity, though perhaps to a lesser degree than anomalous images. Second, in the later time window (800–1500 ms), metaphoric images were expected to elicit a larger positivity than literal images, consistent with additional processing required to construct, evaluate, or integrate a metaphor interpretation once initial semantic access has occurred. Third because the metaphor and literal conditions are matched on familiarity at the group level, we predicted that the metaphor–literal ERP contrast would reflect metaphor-related processing demands rather than familiarity differences. To further evaluate this, we included item-level familiarity as a continuous predictor, expecting that, across all conditions, more familiar images would elicit reduced earlier negativity, reflecting facilitated semantic access for well-known content. In exploratory analyses, we also examined whether item-level sensicality ratings and mean response times predicted amplitude, and whether the strength of these item-level effects differed between an earlier (200–500 ms) and later (500–800 ms) sub-window of the early negativity.

2. Materials and Methods

2.1. Participants

Thirty undergraduate students from the University of Arizona participated in the study. All were native speakers of English, right-handed, and reported normal or corrected-to-normal vision. Participants reported no history of neurological disorders (including seizures or traumatic brain injury) and no current use of psychoactive medication (e.g., for ADHD or depression). Three participants were excluded due to excessive EEG artifacts (>40% of trials rejected), and two were excluded due to low accuracy on attention checks (<80%), resulting in a final sample of 25 participants (17 female; mean age = 21.3 years, SD = 2.6). All participants provided informed consent in accordance with the university’s Institutional Review Board and received course credit for participation.

2.2. Materials

We used a within-participants repeated measures factorial design with three conditions: Literal, Anomalous, and Metaphor. Sixty images were initially selected, and each image was modified using Adobe Photoshop to have three conditions, to control image variables such as how complex or saturated images were. In addition, any text or facial expressions were removed to avoid confounding effects of saliency. Images were also scaled not to exceed 300 pixels in either dimension, to limit their size on the presentation screen. In the metaphor condition, images displayed scenes that contained metaphoric meaning, for example a cracked/broken heart. In the literal condition, images depicted everyday scenes and plausible objects, such as a glass with a visible crack, while in the anomalous condition, images displayed odd, unusual, or unfamiliar scenes and objects that did not contain metaphoric meaning, for example a cracked banana. There was a total of 59 image triplets used in the EEG portion of the study (See Figure 1. for example stimuli). 

2.3 Norming Procedure

For norming, images were divided into three lists using Latin square rotation. Each list contained an equal number of items from all three conditions. Images within each list were presented in a randomized order. Norming participants (N=59) were native speakers of English and participated for course credit. Participants were randomly assigned to one of the three lists and completed the survey online via Qualtrics. 

For each image, participants first described the image in a single sentence. They then rated the image’s sensicality (“Does this image make sense?”) on a 1 (doesn’t make sense) to 4 (makes perfect sense) scale. Next, participants were asked to reflect on the image’s meaning by responding to the prompt: “What do you think the image means? If you cannot make sense of it, type: ‘Nothing.’” Finally, participants rated the familiarity of the image (“How familiar are you with the concept that is represented in the image?”) on a 1 (not at all familiar) to 7 (highly familiar) scale. 

To ensure active participation, attention was verified via 7 images of highly familiar images (e.g., the American flag, Mickey Mouse, Spongebob Squarepants) with specific instructions (e.g, “Please rate as 7”). Participants took on average 60 minutes to complete the norming. Five participants were excluded due to failure of attention checks. One image triplet was excluded because its anomalous image received very high familiarity and sensicality ratings.

Rating results from the remaining 54 participants (Mean Age = 19.9 years, SD = 2.78 years) and 59 triplets showed that anomalous images were low in familiarity (M = 2.77, SD = 0.8) and sensicality (M = 1.92, SD = 0.4), while literal images were high in familiarity (M = 5.46, SD = 0.67) and sensicality (M = 3.35, SD = 0.32). Metaphoric images were more familiar than anomalous images (M = 4.44, SD = 1.07; t(116) = -9.63, p < .001) but less familiar than literal images (t(116) = 6.18, p < .001), as revealed by a paired t-test. Similarly, they were more sensical than anomalous images (M = 2.63, SD = 0.54; t(116) = -8.25, p < .001) but less sensical than literal images (t(116) = 8.76, p <.001), as revealed by a paired t-test. 

Only one image was excluded as a result of sensicality ratings and familiarity ratings were included in the analyses below. 

2.4. EEG Task and Procedure

Participants first completed a short practice phase. The main experiment consisted of three blocks, each corresponding to one stimulus list. The order of these three blocks was counterbalanced across participants using a rotating scheme (e.g., S01: List1–List2–List3; S02: List2–List3–List1; S03: List3–List1–List2). This ensured that each list appeared equally often in each block position. Each list contained items from all three experimental conditions, and stimuli within each block were presented in a randomized order.

Each trial began with a fixation cross for 500 milliseconds, followed by a stimulus image that remained on screen until the participant responded. Participants were instructed to judge whether the image contained a metaphor by pressing a key on the keyboard (A = metaphor; L = no metaphor) first and then to type a brief interpretation of its meaning. Responses were self-paced (median RT = 3,806 ms; IQR = 2,458–6,177 ms). Trials with response times shorter than 1,500 ms were excluded from the ERP analyses to prevent motor artifacts from the button press from contaminating the analysis epoch (7.9% of trials removed). Between trials, a fixation screen prompted them to “blink or continue” to allow rest in order to minimize eye-movement artifacts. Short breaks were provided between blocks.

2.5. Electrophysiological acquisition and processing

Continuous EEG was recorded from 32 active Ag/AgCl electrodes mounted in an elastic cap following the extended 10–20 system (actiCAP, Brain Products, Germany). Signals were amplified using a Brain Products actiCHamp amplifier and digitized at 500 Hz. During recording, Cz served as the online reference, and a forehead electrode served as the ground.  Electrode impedances were kept below 10 kΩ.

EEG preprocessing was performed in MATLAB using EEGLAB 2024 and ERPLAB 2024 (Delorme & Makeig, 2004; Lopez-Calderon & Luck, 2014). Data were filtered with a 0.1 Hz high-pass (2nd-order Butterworth) and a 30 Hz low-pass filter prior to ERP averaging. After filtering, data were then re-referenced offline to the algebraic average of the left and right mastoids. Data were epoched from −200 ms to 1500 ms relative to image onset and baseline-corrected to the −200 ms to 0 ms interval. Channels identified as contaminated based on visual inspection of the continuous data (e.g., channels exhibiting sustained high-frequency noise, flat signal, or extreme drift) were interpolated via spherical splines. Artifact handling followed a two-stage procedure: First, gross artifacts were screened using ERPLAB’s step and moving-window peak-to-peak procedures, and heavily contaminated epochs were excluded. Independent Component Analysis (ICA) was subsequently computed on the pruned data; this computed ICA solution was used for manual component rejection and artifactual components (e.g., ocular/muscle) were removed based on visual inspection.  Second, a stricter post-ICA artifact screening pass was applied (step: 60 µV; moving-window peak-to-peak: 200 µV), and flagged epochs were excluded during ERP averaging. The cleaned, epoched datasets were saved along with an automated QA report summarizing trial counts across stages, presence of ICA, and changes in mean channel Root Mean Square (RMS). 

Epochs were averaged per condition for each participant. For the full stimulus set, average trial counts per condition were 53.4 (SD = 6.01; range = 38–59) for the Literal condition, 54.8 (SD = 4.67; range = 40–59) for the Anomalous condition, and 54.5 (SD = 4.62; range = 39–59) for the Metaphor condition. For the familiarity-matched subset, mean accepted trial counts were 32.2 (SD = 4.93; range = 15–36) for Literal, 33.3 (SD = 3.05; range = 25–36) for Metaphor, and 33.2 (SD = 2.54; range = 26–36) for Anomalous images.

2.6. ERP Analyses

Prior to the ERP analyses, 14 item triplets were excluded based on a qualitative review of behavioral responses. These items were not reliably interpreted as metaphorical by participants in the metaphoric condition (e.g., fewer than 25% of participants provided a metaphor-consistent interpretation) or because their anomalous or literal versions elicited responses inconsistent with their intended condition (e.g., anomalous images frequently interpreted as metaphorical, or literal images frequently misclassified as metaphorical). This left 45 item triplets for the full analyses and 32 familiarity-matched triplets for the condition-matched analyses.

We conducted three ERP analyses. First, using items that were matched in their familiarity ratings by condition (Item N=32 triplets of images), we conducted Repeated-Measures (RM) ANOVAs to examine condition differences. Second, also using familiarity matched items, we conducted mass-univariate cluster-based permutation tests, using all electrodes and time points (sampled at 500 Hz) to identify spatiotemporal clusters of condition differences without predefining regions of interest or time windows. Third, we included the full stimuli (N=45 triplets of images) and conducted an item-level analysis using linear mixed-effects models. This analysis incorporated familiarity as a continuous predictor and included random intercepts for participants and items, allowing familiarity-related variability to be assessed alongside condition effects at the single-trial level.

Repeated Measures ANOVA (Familiarity-matched)

The selections of time windows and electrodes are based both on past literature (see Introduction) and visual inspection (Figure 2). We selected an N300/N400 (200–800 ms) and a Late Positive Component (LPC) (800–1500 ms) at the frontal (F3, Fz, F4), central (C3,Cz, C4), and parietal (P3, Pz, P4) locations.

Fig 2. Grand-average ERP waveforms by condition across scalp regions for matched items.Waveforms are shown for frontal (F3, Fz, F4), central (C3, Cz, C4), and parietal (P3, Pz, P4) regions. Black lines indicate the Literal condition, blue lines the Metaphor condition, and red lines the Anomalous condition.

Mean amplitudes from each condition at each of the location for each participant were entered into separate RM-ANOVA of 3 (Metaphor, Literal, Anomalous) × 3 (Frontal, Central, Parietal) for the two time windows, respectively. Greenhouse–Geisser correction was applied when the assumption of sphericity was violated, meaning that the variances of the differences between pairs of conditions aren’t equal. Planned pairwise comparisons were conducted and false-discovery-rate FDR correction was applied for multiple comparisons. 

Mass-univariate analysis (Familiarity-matched)

We additionally conducted mass-univariate analysis, a cluster-based permutation analysis to identify spatiotemporally extended condition effects without restricting analyses to predefined electrodes or time windows. Mass-univariate ERP analysis tests effects independently at each electrode and time point while controlling the family-wise error rate by evaluating the probability of observing clusters of contiguous significant samples under permutation (Maris & Oostenveld, 2007; Lopez-Calderon & Luck, 2014). 

This analysis was performed using ERPLAB’s FclustGND procedure (10,000 permutations, family-wise error rate α = .05, 75-mm channel neighborhood). The input to this analysis consisted of the preprocessed (artifact rejection, ICA-based correction, re-referencing, and baseline correction to the −200 ms to 0 ms interval) ERP epochs described above, time-locked to image onset (0 ms) and spanning −200 ms to 1500 ms. 

For the mass-univariate analysis, individual participant ERPs were rebinned into an initial set of 18 condition bins reflecting the full factorial design. These ERPs were then merged across participants into a Group Neurophysiology Data (GND) structure, ERPLAB’s group-level data format that stores all subject-level ERPs, trial counts, electrode locations, and timing information in a single matrix suitable for permutation testing (Lopez-Calderon & Luck, 2014). Prior to statistical testing, the data were downsampled from 500 Hz to 125 Hz to reduce computational load and improve cluster stability. To parallel the familiarity-matched ERP analyses, bins were collapsed into matched-familiarity condition bins using trial-count–weighted averaging at the subject level (Literal-Matched, Metaphor-Matched, Anomalous-Matched). Trial counts contributing to each collapsed bin were retained and propagated within the GND structure to ensure accurate weighting and variance estimation. Cluster-based permutation tests were conducted over the full 0 ms – 1500 ms post-stimulus interval, as well as in additional time-restricted analyses aligned to the N300/N400 (200 ms – 800 ms) and LPC (800 ms – 1500 ms) windows. We evaluated a three-level within-subject factor of Condition (Literal, Metaphor, Anomalous) and conducted pairwise contrasts (Metaphor–Literal, Literal–Anomalous, Metaphor–Anomalous). Significant clusters were defined as contiguous spatiotemporal regions exceeding the cluster-level permutation threshold, with cluster significance determined by comparison to the null distribution generated from 10,000 random permutations of condition labels.

Item-level analyses

To quantify item-level variability and assess the contribution of item familiarity to ERP amplitudes, we conducted an item-based analysis using the full stimulus set with unmatched familiarity ratings. Unlike the subject-averaged ERP analysis, this approach explicitly models variability attributable to individual images and participants.

Linear mixed-effects models were fitted to single-trial mean amplitudes extracted from two time windows of interest: the N300/N400 window (200–800 ms) and the Late Positive Component (LPC; 800–1500 ms). Separate models were estimated for each component.

In each model, Condition (Literal, Anomalous, Metaphor) was entered as a categorical fixed effect, and Familiarity was included as a continuous covariate. Familiarity values were obtained from the norming study (See section 2.3) and centered prior to analysis. The Condition × Familiarity interaction was included to test whether familiarity effects differed across image types.

To further examine whether the sustained negativity observed in the 200–800 ms window could be decomposed into temporally distinct processes consistent with Mudrik et al.'s (2010) dual-negativity profile, we additionally fit separate mixed-effects models to single-trial amplitudes extracted from two narrower sub-windows: an earlier window (200–500 ms) and a later window (500–800 ms). These models tested whether item-level familiarity, sensicality (also obtained from the norming study and mean-centered), and item-level mean response time predicted ERP amplitude, and whether any of these effects differed between the two sub-windows. Predictor × Window interactions were tested to formally evaluate whether item-level effects were stronger in the later sub-window. Item-level mean RT was computed by averaging log-transformed response times across subjects for each item within each condition, using only the subjects included in the ERP analyses. Models were fit separately for frontal (F3, Fz, F4), central (C3, Cz, C4), parietal (P3, Pz, P4) ROIs, and their average, with random intercepts for participants and items.

. Models were fit using maximum likelihood estimation. Statistical significance of fixed effects was assessed using Type-III Wald χ² tests, and post-hoc contrasts were evaluated using estimated marginal means with FDR correction for the three pairwise comparisons. .

4. Discussion

This study examined the time course of the processing of visual metaphors with varying degrees of familiarity. There are two main findings across the three analyses of the participant-averaged RM-ANOVAs, the item-level mixed-effects models, and the mass-univariate cluster tests. First, metaphoric images are not simply anomalous images, as reflected by the larger N300/N400 for anomalous than for metaphoric images 200–800 ms after image onset. Second, metaphoric images are differentially processed from literal images, even after item familiarity ratings were matched between the two, as reflected by a larger LPC for metaphoric than literal images.

However, when the full, familiarity-unmatched stimulus set was analyzed using item-level mixed-effects models, familiarity emerged as a strong and reliable predictor of both early and late ERP amplitudes across conditions. In these models, condition differences were  no longer reliable once item-level variability and familiarity were accounted for, indicating that a portion of the variance captured by traditional condition contrasts reflects systematic differences in familiarity across stimulus types.

Together, these results suggest that visual metaphor processing is characterized by both condition-specific neural dynamics and continuous modulation by familiarity. 

4.1. E N300/N400-like negativity reflects anomaly-related processing

Both the RM-ANOVAs and the mass-univariate analysis indicate that the N300/N400-like negativity effect primarily differentiates anomalous content from interpretable images, rather than isolating metaphor-specific mapping. Below, we consider three possible interpretations of this effect: (1) a sustained N400-like response reflecting prolonged semantic integration difficulty, (2) the overlap of multiple components, including an early N300 and a later N700, and (3) a broader negative-going effect that does not map cleanly onto canonical N400 accounts.

First, our negativity effect, despite its long latency from 200 – 800 ms, is essentially the equivalent of the classic N400 effect reported in ERP studies of language for a word that is semantically incongruent with the context in which it occurs in. According to Federmeier and Kutas (2011), such N400 could occur any time between 200 – 600 ms and it indexes the ease/difficulty of semantic retrieval. Under this account, when our participants viewed the images, which were complex, it might have taken them some extra time (i.e., longer) to retrieve the meanings of multiple visual elements from the long-term memory in the anomalous images than in the literal images.  

Importantly, prior work on visual metaphor does not provide strong evidence for a single sustained component spanning this entire interval as we found here. For example, Ortiz et al. (2017) reported discrete differences between hybrid metaphors and literal images at multiple, narrowly defined time windows, including an N400-range effect (406–430 ms) and a later positivity (580–587 ms), rather than a continuous sustained negativity. Their findings support the idea that visual metaphors engage multiple temporally distinct processes, but they do not uniquely motivate a sustained effect. Related ERP work on pictorial and verbal-pictorial metaphors in advertising and multimodal designs similarly reports N400-range effects and later positivities associated with integration and evaluative processing demands, though stimulus control and task differences make direct mapping across paradigms nontrivial (Cao et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2023).The present results, which show a broader and more sustained negative-going effect, likely reflect differences in task demands and analytic approach, as participants here were required not only to judge metaphoricity but also to actively generate an interpretation, which is in line with reports of extended ERP effects when the task requires continued or deep processing.

A second possibility is that the broad negativity observed here reflects the temporal overlap of multiple functionally distinct components rather than a single sustained process. In the visual processing literature, negativities in the 250–350 ms range (the N300) have been linked to the initial mapping of visual input onto stored object representations, essentially, the difficulty of recognizing what is depicted (McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; Hamm et al., 2002). The subsequent N400 (350–500 ms) is thought to reflect the integration of recognized objects with broader semantic context and expectations. Beyond these, later negative-going activity has been associated with imagery, mental simulation, and elaborative conceptual processing (West & Holcomb, 2000). Most directly relevant, Mudrik et al. (2010) reported that simultaneously viewed incongruent scenes elicited a broadly distributed negativity between 650 and 850 ms, following an earlier N300/N400-like effect. Because their paradigm shares the critical feature of presenting a single complex image requiring interpretation, rather than a sequential verbal or pictorial stream that would support incremental prediction, their results provide the closest precedent for the sustained negativity observed here. Under this account, anomalous images may have elicited an early N300 because the depicted objects were difficult to categorize, followed by an N400 reflecting failed semantic integration, and finally sustained negativity reflecting continued attempts to construct a coherent interpretation. The broad 200–800 ms window used in the present analysis would capture all three of these overlapping processes as a single effect. This interpretation is consistent with the mass-univariate results, which showed that the Metaphor–Anomalous cluster began earlier (326 ms) and was larger than the Literal–Anomalous cluster (onset at 454 ms), potentially reflecting differential engagement of these sub-processes across conditions. However, our item-level analyses of the earlier (200–500 ms) and later (500–800 ms) sub-windows argue against a clean separation between two discrete components. Item-level familiarity and sensicality significantly predicted amplitude in both sub-windows, and although their effects were numerically larger in the later sub-window, the predictor × window interactions did not reach significance. This pattern is more consistent with a graded, sustained response to item interpretability than with two functionally distinct components that should, in principle, be differentially sensitive to different item properties. One observation does hint at some functional differentiation between the sub-windows: item-level mean RT explained unique variance in the 500–800 ms sub-window even after familiarity and sensicality were accounted for, whereas it did not in the 200–500 ms sub-window. This suggests that the later portion of the negativity may reflect processing demands that go beyond what is captured by static item ratings alone, a pattern broadly consistent with Mudrik et al.'s (2010) interpretation of their 650–850 ms negativity as reflecting late semantic evaluation and response preparation. However, the overall pattern of item-level effects is more consistent with a continuous, prolonged response than with two cleanly dissociable components. Distinguishing among these possibilities will likely require methods with finer temporal and functional resolution than averaged ERPs, such as time-frequency decomposition or temporal decoding.

On balance, the item-level pattern favors interpretation of the 200–800 ms negativity as a prolonged, graded response to item interpretability; an extended version of the sustained picture-evoked negativities described by Mudrik et al. (2010); rather than a clean sequence of functionally independent N300, N400, and N700 components. 

Third, it is worth acknowledging that the negativity observed here may not map cleanly onto any single canonical component. The N300, N400, and N700 were originally characterized in paradigms with simpler stimuli, single objects in scenes, word pairs, or sequential comic panels, where processing unfolds in a more constrained fashion. Hybrid visual metaphors present a qualitatively different challenge: viewers encounter a single, complex, composite image that simultaneously activates multiple object representations and demands a coherent interpretation. Under these conditions, the observed negativity may reflect a cascade of partially overlapping processes ;recognition, integration, and elaboration, that are difficult to decompose with the temporal resolution of averaged ERPs. The distinction between interpretations (1) and (2) may ultimately require complementary methods such as time-frequency analysis or temporal decoding to resolve.

Importantly, regardless of which of these three accounts best characterizes the underlying processes, the pattern aligns across our three analytic approaches. In the familiarity-matched participant-averaged analysis and the mass-univariate analysis, early differences were driven by anomaly-related mismatch rather than metaphorical mapping per se. In contrast, the item-level mixed-effects analysis demonstrated that familiarity independently modulates early amplitudes across conditions, indicating that familiarity contributes to the magnitude of this negativity but does not account for the categorical anomalous-versus-interpretable distinction observed when familiarity is controlled. Together, these results suggest that the early sustained negativity reflects a combination of anomaly-driven semantic mismatch and graded item-level influences on visual meaning construction, rather than a metaphor-specific effect.The topographic time course (Figure 5) is consistent with this graded interpretation: the Ano–Lit and Ano–Met negativities build continuously from approximately 300 ms onward and reach their maximum amplitude between 600 and 900 ms, without the clear return-to-baseline between 400 and 600 ms that would be expected if two functionally distinct components (an early N300/N400 and a later N700) were driving the effect.

4.2. Late positivity and metaphor-related meaning construction

In the later time window (800–1500 ms), metaphoric images elicited a larger positivity than both literal and anomalous images in the participant-averaged analysis. Mass-univariate results converged with and extended this finding: the Metaphor–Literal contrast produced a sustained positive-going cluster from approximately 942–1486 ms, while the Metaphor–Anomalous contrast yielded a broad cluster beginning around 798 ms and persisting through 1486 ms. Together, these findings indicate that metaphor-specific differentiation emerged most robustly in later activity.

In the linguistic metaphor literature, later positivities such as the P600 or Late Positive Component are not obligatorily elicited by metaphors, but are reliably observed under conditions that require extended interpretation, reanalysis, or evaluation of meaning. Specifically, late positivities are more likely to emerge for novel, weakly supported, or contextually underspecified metaphors, whereas highly conventional metaphors may elicit minimal or no late positivity and instead show primarily N400 modulation (Kuperberg, 2007; De Grauwe et al., 2010; Van Petten & Luka, 2012; Bambini et al., 2016). Thus, later positivities are best understood as indexing downstream interpretive operations engaged when meaning is not immediately resolved, rather than serving as a metaphor-specific signature per se.

A similar pattern has been reported in visual processing. Late positivities are commonly observed in picture-based ERP paradigms when comprehenders must integrate scene structure, revise an evolving event representation, or update an initial interpretation. In studies of visual narratives and scene comprehension, late ERP effects have been reported in multiple time ranges. Positive-going activity emerging after approximately 600–900 ms has been linked to post-recognition integration and updating processes, particularly when the visual input violates expectations or requires additional inferential work (Cohn et al., 2012; Võ & Wolfe, 2013; Cohn & Kuperberg, 2014; Coderre et al., 2020). In some studies, these effects extend even later: Cohn (2021) analyzed positivities in an 800–1,100 ms window, and Mudrik et al. (2010) reported a late negativity from 650–850 ms associated with semantic evaluation and response preparation. It is worth noting that in the linguistic metaphor literature, Baiocco et al. (2026) found that 37.1% of reviewed experiments reported a late negativity (LN) in the 500–1,000 ms range, more commonly than the late positivity (20.9%), particularly for novel metaphors and tasks requiring deeper evaluation. Whether analogous late negativities occur for visual metaphors has not been tested." The present study used a broader late window (800–1,500 ms) than these prior investigations, which may have contributed to the sustained and widespread nature of the clusters observed here. However, the mass-univariate analysis, which does not rely on predefined windows, independently confirmed that metaphor-related positivity extended through the end of the epoch, suggesting that the effect is not an artifact of window selection. Thus, our findings suggest that prior studies may have unintentionally “cut off” time of continued processing by choosing shorter timewindows or inter stimulus intervals.

The present task was explicitly designed to engage such processes. Participants were required not only to judge whether an image was metaphoric, but also to commit to an interpretation by generating a written explanation. This task structure likely amplified downstream evaluation and integration demands relative to passive viewing, thereby enhancing late positivity effects. Under these conditions, metaphoric images require mapping depicted visual features onto an abstract target concept, a process that goes beyond basic object recognition or plausibility assessment.

Importantly, the late Metaphor–Literal effect was observed in the familiarity-matched subset, reducing the likelihood that this positivity reflects residual familiarity differences between conditions. Moreover, the absence of a Condition × location interaction indicates that the effect was broadly distributed across the scalp, consistent with sustained, task-relevant interpretive processing rather than a sharply localized perceptual or semantic response. Taken together, these findings support the interpretation that the observed late positivity reflects downstream meaning construction and evaluative processes engaged during visual metaphor comprehension, rather than early semantic access per se.

4.3. Familiarity effects across time windows

The item-level mixed-effects models indicated that familiarity predicted ERP amplitudes in both time windows, with no reliable Condition × Familiarity interaction. In the 200–800 ms window, each one-point increase in familiarity was associated with a +1.38 µV increase in amplitude, indicating that less familiar images elicited more negative responses in this interval. In the LPC window (800–1500 ms), the same direction of effect was observed (β = +1.06 µV per point). The absence of a Condition × Familiarity interaction suggests that familiarity modulated responses similarly across literal, anomalous, and metaphoric images, consistent with familiarity influencing general efficiency of accessing and integrating visual meaning rather than selectively altering metaphor processing. This pattern mirrors findings in the linguistic metaphor literature, where novel metaphors reliably elicit larger N400 amplitudes than conventional ones (Arzouan et al., 2007; Lai et al., 2009; Bambini et al., 2016), and the recent meta-analytic synthesis by Baiocco et al. (2026) showing that an N400 effect emerges in 84.8% of experiments with novel metaphors but only 54.5% of those with conventional metaphors. Our data suggest that this modulation is not language-specific: familiarity exerts the same graded influence on visual semantic processing, consistent with the view that familiarity operates at a conceptual level largely independent of presentation modality (Hodiamont et al., 2018; Lakoff & Johnson, 1980).

The sub-window analyses refined this picture further. When familiarity and sensicality were modeled together across the 200–500 and 500–800 ms sub-windows, both remained significant predictors of amplitude and continued to explain variance even after condition was included in the model. Item-level mean response time showed the same pattern and additionally contributed unique variance specifically in the 500–800 ms sub-window. The continuity of these effects across sub-windows is theoretically informative: rather than observing a familiarity effect that peaks in a narrow canonical N400 window and then dissipates, we see a graded response that persists throughout the early sustained negativity. This fits with Mudrik et al.'s (2010) observation that visual scene processing generates both an early N300/N400-like negativity and a later, broadly distributed negativity for incongruent stimuli, and with the broader point that picture-evoked negativities often span wider time ranges than the canonical linguistic N400 (McPherson & Holcomb, 1999; West & Holcomb, 2000). The additional contribution of item-level RT in the 500–800 ms sub-window suggests that later activity captures processing demands not fully reducible to static ratings, possibly reflecting the ongoing effort of constructing a coherent interpretation, a process that plausibly scales with how long a viewer ultimately takes to commit to an answer.

The two time windows nevertheless behaved differently with respect to condition. In the 200–800 ms window, adding familiarity to the model rendered the condition effect non-significant, indicating that much of the variance attributed to condition in this window is shared with item-level familiarity differences. By contrast, in the LPC window (800–1500 ms), condition remained a robust predictor even after familiarity was accounted for. This dissociation provides the strongest evidence that the late metaphor effect is not reducible to familiarity: the early negativity tracks with how familiar or interpretable an image is, regardless of condition, whereas the later positivity reflects condition-specific processing demands that persist after familiarity is controlled. The familiarity-matched participant-averaged analysis converges on this conclusion: the Metaphor–Literal LPC difference was significant in the matched subset where familiarity was equated by design. This asymmetry has an important methodological implication for the visual metaphor ERP literature. Prior studies comparing metaphoric and literal images without matching or modeling familiarity (e.g., Ortiz et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2018) may have conflated genuine metaphor-related processing with expectancy-driven variation, particularly in early time windows. Our results suggest that the LPC is the more robust locus of metaphor-specific differentiation, and that any claim about metaphor-related N400 modulation in the visual modality should be evaluated against item-level familiarity controls.

4.4. Behavioral performance and task demands

Behaviorally, participants showed moderate discriminability and a conservative bias toward “not metaphor.” Hit rates for metaphor trials were lower than correct rejections for non-metaphor trials, indicating that explicit metaphor categorization was not uniformly easy across items. This performance profile fits the neural pattern: early activity primarily separated anomalous from interpretable images, whereas later activity differentiated metaphoric from literal images, consistent with additional processing associated with arriving at, evaluating, or committing to a metaphor interpretation. The requirement to provide an interpretation likely promoted extended processing and may help explain why metaphor effects were strongest in the late positivity window. 

This conservative bias increased over the course of the experiment. In the three-condition design, the expected base rate of "not metaphor" responses is two-thirds, given that two of the three conditions are non-metaphoric. In the first block, the observed rate closely matched this expectation, but "not metaphor" responses rose reliably across successive blocks, suggesting that participants became increasingly reluctant to commit to a metaphor judgment as the task progressed. This shift may reflect the cumulative cost of the interpretive demand: because selecting "metaphor" required typing an explanation, participants may have increasingly favored the less effortful response. Response times were also longer than in typical forced-choice ERP paradigms (median = 3,806 ms; IQR = 2,458–6,177 ms), likely reflecting this additional interpretive burden. 

4.5. Conclusions

This study examined the time course of hybrid visual metaphor processing while directly modeling item-level familiarity, a factor that is frequently confounded with metaphoricity in both linguistic and visual paradigms. Three conclusions follow.

Firstly, early negativities from 200–800 ms indexed the interpretability of the visual input rather than metaphoric mapping. This suggests that visual anomaly detection and metaphor comprehension are dissociable at the neural level, with anomaly engaging earlier semantic mechanisms and metaphor engaging later interpretive ones.. Across the familiarity-matched participant-averaged analysis and the mass-univariate tests, anomalous images elicited larger negativities than both literal and metaphoric images, with no reliable Metaphor–Literal difference. This pattern aligns with picture-based ERP work showing that semantically incoherent or difficult-to-integrate visual inputs elicit extended negativities in the N300/N400 range, especially under tasks that encourage continued interpretation rather than passive detection. In other words, the early window in this paradigm behaved more like a marker of failed or effortful visual meaning construction than a signature of metaphoric mapping per se.

Second, metaphor-specific differentiation emerged most clearly in later activity. Metaphoric images elicited a broad late positivity relative to literal images, and this effect persisted into an extended window in the mass-univariate analysis. This timing is consistent with the literature reviewed in the introduction: later positivities (P600/LPC/LPC-like effects) are commonly observed when comprehenders must go beyond initial access and engage in interpretive evaluation, integration, or selection among candidate meanings, rather than simply detecting semantic mismatch. Importantly, the late Metaphor–Literal effect was present in the familiarity-matched subset, strengthening the inference that it cannot be reduced to systematic familiarity differences between metaphoric and literal items. Thus, the later time window in this study likely reflects continued meaning construction and evaluation. 

Third, familiarity exerted a robust, modality-general influence across the processing stream. In mixed-effects models on the full stimulus set, familiarity predicted both early and late amplitudes without a reliable Condition × Familiarity interaction, indicating that familiarity modulates general efficiency of accessing and integrating visual meaning rather than selectively altering metaphor processing. Methodologically, this is a key takeaway for the visual metaphor ERP literature: without item-level familiarity measurement and modeling, condition differences may conflate metaphoricity with graded differences in expectancy, plausibility, and prior experience.

Taken together, these findings support a hybrid account of what “metaphor ERPs” reflect in the visual domain. The components observed here resemble canonical language-linked components in their timing and polarity, but the functional interpretation is better framed in terms of domain-general semantic and interpretive mechanisms rather than language-specific processes. The absence of a Metaphor–Literal effect in the early window suggests that metaphoric mapping does not necessarily add early semantic access difficulty beyond what is required for recognizing an interpretable scene. By contrast, the reliable late positivity suggests that visual metaphors engage downstream meaning construction and evaluative operations that are plausibly modality-general and recruited when an interpretation must be constructed, selected, and committed to. At the same time, these data do not rule out metaphor-specific computations; rather, they suggest that any such computations are most evident in later, post-recognition stages under tasks that demand explicit interpretation.

Several directions follow for future work. A priority is to more tightly dissociate metaphoric mapping from general anomaly/incoherence by constructing stimulus sets in which metaphor and literal items are matched not only on familiarity but also on plausibility, visual complexity, and interpretability, and by including non-metaphoric but meaning-rich controls (e.g., novel literal scenes) to test whether late positivities are selective to metaphor versus any difficult interpretation. Second, manipulating task demands (passive viewing vs categorization vs interpretation generation) would clarify whether the late positivity scales with explicit interpretive commitment, as suggested by both visual narrative work and the present pattern. Third, combining ERPs with additional measures such as such as eye-tracking could clarify the time course of visual inspection during metaphor processing, though whether such methods can distinguish among the cognitive sub-processes underlying late positivities remains an open question. . Finally, increasing the range of visual metaphor types (hybrid vs contextual vs multimodal/verbo-pictorial metaphors) would allow stronger claims about generality across the heterogeneous class of visual metaphors discussed in theoretical frameworks.

Overall, this study contributes a controlled, time-resolved characterization of visual metaphor comprehension that bridges the visual meaning construction literature and the metaphor ERP literature. By showing that anomaly drives early negativities while metaphor-specific differentiation emerges most strongly in later positivity under familiarity control, the findings sharpen what ERP components can and cannot be taken to mean in visual metaphor research and motivate item-level modeling as a baseline standard for future electrophysiological studies of metaphoric meaning. These findings may also carry implications for the linguistic metaphor ERP literature: if familiarity modulates early negativities similarly across modalities, then the N400 effects widely reported for verbal metaphors (Baiocco et al., 2026) may likewise reflect graded differences in familiarity or expectancy rather than metaphoric mapping per se, a possibility that item-level modeling could help resolve in future linguistic studies.
